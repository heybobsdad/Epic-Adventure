Horsethief Lake — Day Trip 3
The quietest and most underrated day in the Black Hills. Wind Cave National Park sits at the southern edge of the region — a vast mixed-grass prairie above ground and one of the longest, most complex cave systems in the world below it. Wind Cave is 35 miles from camp. Driving back to drop Max off and return would add 70 miles and over an hour to the day. Max stays at camp. You go clean.

Prairie Drives  ·  1–1.5 hrs
Wind Cave National Park — Prairie Drives

Wind Cave National Park protects 30,000 acres of one of the last remaining intact mixed-grass prairies in North America. Most visitors come for the cave and miss what’s above ground entirely — which is their loss. The park’s gravel roads NPS 5 and NPS 6 wind through the eastern section of the park through open grassland and ponderosa pine forest, and the wildlife density here regularly exceeds what you see in Custer State Park or the Badlands. Bison, elk, pronghorn, deer, prairie dogs, and coyotes all roam this landscape with relatively little human pressure.

What to Expect

Drive slowly, pull over at every movement in the grass. Bison herds here are frequently larger and less habituated to vehicles than those on the Wildlife Loop — they move where they want, when they want. More than one visitor has reported seeing more wildlife in an hour on these quiet gravel roads than in an entire day elsewhere in the Black Hills. The park entrance is free. No fee to drive the surface roads.

Rankin Ridge Overlook

A short drive north of the visitor center, Rankin Ridge is the highest point in the park at 5,013 feet. The short nature trail to the fire tower (1 mile loop) offers sweeping views of the Black Hills transition zone where forest meets prairie. Early morning light on the grasslands from this vantage point is exceptional.

Cave Tour — Optional  ·  1–1.5 hrs
Wind Cave — Ranger-Guided Cave Tour

Wind Cave is the seventh longest cave in the world and home to the highest known concentration of boxwork formations on earth — delicate honeycomb-like calcite structures that form on almost no other cave walls anywhere. The cave also breathes: barometric pressure differences between underground and surface cause air to flow in and out of the natural entrance, which is how the Lakota discovered it centuries before European explorers arrived. All cave access is by ranger-guided tour only.

Book in Advance

Tours sell out days to weeks ahead during summer. Book at recreation.gov before you arrive. The Garden of Eden tour (1 hour, moderate) is the best first visit — accessible passages, excellent boxwork displays, manageable stairs. The cave maintains a constant 54°F year-round. Bring a light layer regardless of the temperature outside.

Max

Max stays at camp for the cave tour. One of you goes in while the other stays with him — or book early enough that you can stagger two tours. The visitor center parking area is shaded and comfortable for a wait.

Afternoon Wildlife Loop  ·  1.5–2 hrs
Custer State Park — Afternoon on the Wildlife Loop

Yes — you drove this loop on Day 1. You’re driving it again, and it’s intentional. Wind Cave’s Blue Bell entrance connects directly to the Wildlife Loop Road about 6 miles north, making it a natural close to the day. But the better reason is this: the loop looks completely different in late afternoon than it did in midday light. The bison herds move. The Begging Burros work a different section of road. The pronghorn appear in fields you drove past earlier without seeing anything. Late-day light on open grassland is its own reward, and the Wildlife Loop earns a second pass.

Heading Home

The Blue Bell entrance puts you on US-16A heading north — about 22 miles back to Horsethief Lake, roughly 40 minutes. Wind Cave and Custer combined make for a full, quiet day in the southern Black Hills. The kind of day that doesn’t feel rushed until you realize it’s nearly dark.
